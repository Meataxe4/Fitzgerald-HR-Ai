What Is the Hospitality Award in Australia?

The Hospitality Industry (General) Award MA000009 is the primary modern award covering hospitality businesses in Australia. It sets the minimum pay rates, MA000009 penalty rates, allowances, and working conditions for employees in pubs, restaurants, cafes, bars, hotels, and catering operations under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth). It is one of the most complex modern awards in the Australian employment law framework, with flat-dollar loadings, split shift provisions, and casual conversion obligations that differ significantly from other Awards.

Classification Levels — How Employees Are Classified Under MA000009

Under the Hospitality Award MA000009, employees are classified based on their role, skill level, and responsibilities. Each classification level determines the minimum pay rate that applies. Misclassifying employees is one of the most common — and most expensive — compliance errors in Australian hospitality venues.

Classification levels under MA000009 include:

  • Level 1: Introductory/entry-level roles with minimal experience — typically the first few months of employment before progression
  • Level 2: Food and beverage attendants, kitchen hands, and equivalent roles — the most common classification in restaurants, cafes, and pubs
  • Level 3: Employees with trade qualifications or equivalent skills, including experienced cooks and senior food and beverage roles
  • Level 4–6: Specialist, supervisory, and management-equivalent classifications with higher minimum rates and additional entitlements

Award Coverage — Who It Applies To

The Hospitality Award covers employees working in:

  • Hotels, motels, and serviced apartments
  • Restaurants, cafes, and coffee shops
  • Pubs, bars, and licensed clubs (not covered by another Award)
  • Catering operations
  • Functions and events venues

It does not cover employees covered by the Restaurant Industry Award MA000119, or those working under a registered enterprise agreement that covers the same conditions. Common Hospitality Award Mistakes That Cost Venues Money

The following errors are the most frequently identified in Fair Work Ombudsman investigations of Australian hospitality venues. Each is a separate contravention with its own penalty exposure:

  • Applying evening and night loadings as percentages — the $2.81/hr and $4.22/hr loadings are flat dollar amounts under MA000009, not percentages of the ordinary rate. Applying them as percentages systematically underpays lower-classification employees and overpays higher-classification employees
  • Failing to offer casual conversion at 12 months — a written offer or written reasons must be provided to eligible casuals at the 12-month threshold. Missing this is a specific MA000009 requirement contravention, separate from any underpayment
  • Incorrect employee classification — classifying employees below their actual skill and responsibility level creates ongoing underpayment exposure across every shift worked
  • Recording rostered hours rather than actual hours — record-keeping obligations under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) require actual start and finish times. Rostered hours are not sufficient
  • Absorbing casual loading into a flat rate — the 25% casual loading under MA000009 must be applied on top of the ordinary rate and all applicable penalty rates. Absorbing it into a single flat rate may still constitute underpayment when penalty rates apply
  • Applying the wrong Award entirely — confusing MA000009 with the Restaurant Industry Award MA000119 is a foundational error that compounds across every payroll cycle. See Hospitality vs Restaurant Award — key differences

Fair Work Compliance — What Venues Must Do

Operating under the Hospitality Award MA000009 creates specific compliance obligations for hospitality businesses in Australia beyond just paying the correct rates:

  • Record keeping: All employee records must be kept for 7 years, including actual start and finish times (not rostered times), pay rates, leave records, and superannuation contributions
  • Payslips: Must be issued within one working day of pay day, and must include all required information under the Fair Work Regulations
  • Casual conversion: Written offer or written reasons must be provided to eligible casuals at the 12-month mark
  • Overtime: Applies after ordinary hours limits — must be paid at the correct overtime rate, not absorbed into a flat hourly rate

What are the penalty rates under the Hospitality Award in 2026?
Saturday: 150% (full-time/part-time), 175% (casual). Sunday: 175% (full-time/part-time), 200% (casual). Public Holiday: 250% (full-time/part-time), 275% (casual). When must casual employees be offered conversion under the Hospitality Award?
After 12 months of regular and systematic employment. The employer must make a written offer of conversion or provide written reasons why conversion is not appropriate. Failing to do so is a specific contravention under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth), with each affected employee constituting a separate count. See the complete casual conversion guide.
What is the split shift allowance under the Hospitality Award?
A flat dollar allowance payable per day that a split shift is worked — where there is a break between periods of duty exceeding the meal break. The maximum spread of hours must not exceed the Award limit without additional entitlements applying. See the split shift rules guide.

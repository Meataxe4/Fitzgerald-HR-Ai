Casual Conversion Rules Under the Hospitality Award — What Venue Owners Must Know

8 Mar 2026 Updated 7 Apr 2026 By Fitz HR 6 min read

Casual conversion is one of the most commonly overlooked compliance obligations in Australian hospitality — and one of the most penalised. Most venue owners don't realise they've missed it until it's too late. If you employ casual staff, the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) requires you to act at the 12-month mark. Missing that deadline is not an oversight — it's a contravention.

What Is Casual Conversion?

Casual conversion is the right of a casual employee to be offered permanent (full-time or part-time) employment after they have been employed for 12 months and have worked a regular pattern of hours for at least the last 6 months — a pattern that could reasonably continue as a permanent arrangement.

This is not optional and it is not discretionary. It is a legal obligation under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth), and it applies to all employers covered by the Hospitality Industry (General) Award MA000009. Most hospitality venues employ high numbers of casuals. That means most hospitality venues have casual conversion obligations they need to be actively managing.

The Core Obligation

Within 21 days of a casual employee reaching 12 months of service, you must either offer them conversion to permanent employment — or provide written notice explaining why you are not. Silence is not an option — and it's one of the most common breaches.

Who Is Eligible?

A casual employee is eligible for conversion if they have been employed for at least 12 months, and during the last 6 of those months they have worked a regular pattern of hours that could continue as a permanent arrangement. "Regular pattern" doesn't mean identical shifts every week — it means a sufficiently consistent pattern that could reasonably be performed on a permanent basis.

Who is not eligible

An employee who has had genuinely irregular hours — different days each week, highly variable hours with no consistent pattern — would generally not meet the eligibility threshold. However, venue owners should be careful about assuming irregularity. If in doubt, seek advice before declining to offer conversion — the risk of getting this wrong sits with you, not the employee.

Your Obligations — Step by Step

Month 11
Review the employee's work pattern

Assess whether the employee has worked a regular pattern of hours for the last 6 months. Pull their timesheets. Determine whether they are eligible before the deadline arrives.

Day 1 of Month 13
21-day window opens

From the date the employee reaches 12 months of service, you have 21 days to issue either an offer of conversion or a written notice declining to offer. The clock is running.

Within 21 Days
Issue the offer or the notice

If offering conversion: specify the type of employment (full-time or part-time), the proposed hours, and the start date. If declining: provide written reasons that are lawful and documented.

Employee Response
Allow 21 days to respond

The employee has 21 days to accept or decline the offer. If they decline, they remain casual. If they accept, convert them to permanent employment from the agreed date.

When Can You Refuse to Offer?

You can decline to offer conversion — but only on specific, documented grounds. Reasonable grounds under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) include:

  • The employee's position will cease to exist in the next 12 months
  • The hours the employee has been working will be significantly reduced in the next 12 months
  • There will be a significant change in the days or times worked in the next 12 months that cannot be accommodated in a permanent arrangement

"I prefer to keep them casual" is not a reasonable ground. Neither is "it's more flexible for us." The grounds must be genuine, specific, and documented in the written notice you provide to the employee.

What Happens if You Miss the Deadline?

Failing to comply with casual conversion obligations is actively targeted by the Fair Work Ombudsman — particularly in hospitality, where casual employment is prevalent and conversion obligations are frequently missed.

The maximum penalty is $93,900 per contravention for a company — and each employee whose conversion right was missed is a separate contravention. A venue with five eligible casuals that misses all five deadlines faces potential penalties of up to $469,500. Beyond fines, affected employees may be entitled to back-payment of leave entitlements they would have accrued as permanent staff.

These are not theoretical penalties — they are actively enforced in hospitality.

What Not to Do

These are the mistakes that turn a missed deadline into a significant liability:

✗
Don't assume your casual employees don't qualify. The eligibility threshold is lower than most venue owners expect. If an employee has been working similar hours across a 6-month period, they may well be eligible — regardless of minor week-to-week variation.
✗
Don't rely on verbal conversations. The obligation is to provide written notice — both offers and refusals. A verbal discussion is not compliant. If it's not in writing, it didn't happen.
✗
Don't cut an employee's hours around the 12-month mark to break their pattern. This is the most common avoidance strategy — and the most scrutinised. Deliberately reducing hours to avoid a conversion obligation is a separate breach and significantly increases legal exposure.
✗
Don't confuse "small business" with "exempt." Since March 2021, all employers must offer conversion. Small businesses are not exempt — they must respond to employee-initiated requests, and the employer-initiated obligation applies once the employee has worked 12 months.

How to Stay Compliant

1
Maintain a casual employment register

Record every casual employee's start date. Set a reminder at 11 months so you have time to assess eligibility before the deadline.

2
Review timesheets at the 11-month mark

Look at the last 6 months of the employee's work pattern. Is it regular enough to constitute an eligible pattern? Document your assessment.

3
Issue the written offer or notice

Within 21 days of the 12-month anniversary: either a compliant written offer of conversion or a written notice declining with specific, documented reasons.

4
Keep copies of everything

Store the offer or notice, any employee response, and your internal assessment. If the Fair Work Ombudsman comes asking, this documentation is your first line of defence.

Frequently Asked Questions

When do I have to offer casual conversion in Australia?
Within 21 days of a casual employee reaching 12 months of service. This obligation applies to all employers covered by the Hospitality Industry (General) Award MA000009 under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth).
What happens if I don't offer casual conversion?
You may face penalties under the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth), and affected employees may claim entitlements they missed as permanent staff. The maximum penalty is $93,900 per contravention for a company — and each missed employee is a separate contravention. Affected employees may also be entitled to back-payment of leave entitlements they would have accrued as permanent staff. The Fair Work Ombudsman actively targets this in hospitality.
Can an employer refuse casual conversion in Australia?
Yes — but only on reasonable business grounds that are genuine, specific, and documented. Reasonable grounds include: the position will cease to exist, hours will significantly reduce, or the work pattern will change substantially. You must provide written notice explaining the specific reason. "I prefer casuals" is not a lawful ground.
Does casual conversion apply to small businesses?
Yes. Small businesses must still respond to employee requests and comply with the legislation. Since 27 March 2021, casual conversion rights apply universally under the Fair Work Act 2009. Small businesses are not exempt from responding to employee-initiated conversion requests.
Can I reduce a casual's hours to avoid conversion?
No. Deliberately altering hours to avoid conversion obligations can itself be a breach of the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth). Deliberately reducing hours around the 12-month mark to break an employee's regular pattern is one of the most common avoidance strategies — and one of the fastest ways to escalate a minor issue into a serious breach of the Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth).

This is exactly the kind of obligation that gets missed when you're running a busy venue — not because of bad intent, but because no one set a reminder. The cost of missing it is disproportionate to the effort required to comply.
